Concept

Close Your Eyes for a Second

Okay don't actually close them, you need to read this.

But imagine you're sitting at your computer, bored, and a webpage pops up.

It says: "CONGRATULATIONS! You are our 1,000,000th visitor! Click the button below to claim your free AirPods!"

There's a big shiny button in the middle of the screen.

You think — okay, probably fake, but why not, let me just click it.

You click.

Nothing happens. No AirPods. Weird.

But then, three days later, you get an email from your bank.

"You have successfully transferred $5,000 to an unknown account."

You never went to your bank's website. You never authorized a transfer. You were just trying to win fake AirPods.

What just happened?

Concept

Here's What Actually Happened

That "claim your prize" webpage had two layers.

The layer you could see — the congratulations message and the fake button.

And a layer you could not see — completely invisible, sitting right on top of that fake button. An invisible copy of your bank's "Confirm Transfer" button, positioned pixel-perfectly underneath your mouse.

When you clicked what you thought was the prize button, you actually clicked the invisible bank button underneath.

Your browser didn't know anything was wrong. You clicked something. The thing at that location received the click. Done.

That's Clickjacking.

You weren't hacked. Your computer wasn't infected. The attacker just put an invisible thing exactly where they knew you'd click.

Concept

The Thing That Makes This Possible — iframes

In HTML, there's something called an iframe. Think of it like a picture frame you hang on a wall — except instead of a painting inside it, there's a live, fully working other website playing inside it.

You've seen this before without realizing it. When you watch a YouTube video that's embedded inside a news article — that little video player isn't part of the news site. It's a YouTube page, loaded inside a rectangular frame on the news page.

That rectangle is an iframe.

Now here's the key detail that makes clickjacking possible.

You can have two iframes stacked on top of each other.

And you can make the top one completely invisible.

Concept

The Invisible Glass Layer

Imagine a fish tank.

Inside the fish tank there's a real fish swimming around. That's your bank's website — the bottom iframe. Fully real, fully functional.

Now imagine someone places a pane of perfectly clear glass directly on top of the fish tank's opening. You can still see the fish. You can't see the glass. But if you try to reach in to touch the fish, your hand hits the glass first.

That invisible glass is the attacker's top iframe.

In CSS, the attacker sets opacity: 0 on the top iframe. Zero opacity means completely transparent — your eyes go right through it. But your click lands on it first.

The browser doesn't care what you can see. It cares what's on top. And the top layer always — always — receives the click.

Ajan said this in the lecture and it's the single most important thing about clickjacking: The top layer is always clickable.
Concept

How the Attacker Aims the Invisible Layer

Okay so now the attacker has two iframes. One visible real page, one invisible trap page.

But they need to position the invisible one exactly right — so your click lands on the button they want you to press.

Web pages use a coordinate system. Think of it like a map of the page.

The very top-left corner of any webpage is position zero, zero. That's the starting point.

top means how many pixels down from that corner.

left means how many pixels to the right.

So if a button on your bank's page sits at "top 400 pixels, left 200 pixels" — the attacker adjusts their invisible iframe until it lines up at exactly that same spot.

They basically slide the invisible glass pane around until the invisible "confirm transfer" button sits right on top of the shiny fake "claim prize" button below.

Then they test it. Click it themselves a few times. Adjust by a few pixels. Test again. Until it's perfect.

Concept

The Stacking Problem — Who Gets to Be on Top?

When two iframes overlap, the browser needs to decide which one is actually on top.

There are two ways this is controlled.

Way 1 — z-index. This is a CSS number that represents depth. Think of it like the floors of a building. If iframe A is on floor 1 and iframe B is on floor 10, iframe B is higher up and covers iframe A. The attacker gives their invisible iframe a higher z-index than the real page underneath.

Way 2 — Declaration order. If nobody sets a z-index at all, the browser falls back to a simple rule. Whichever iframe is written last in the HTML code sits on top. It's like stacking sheets of paper — the last one you put down is on top of the pile.

The attacker uses one of these to guarantee their invisible iframe is always the one that catches the click.

Impact

What Can an Attacker Actually Make You Do?

Once you understand the trick — invisible layer on top, real button underneath — you can imagine all the damage.

01 — Social
Likejacking
You think you're clicking a game button. The invisible layer is a Facebook "Like" button for a hate group. You just publicly liked something you'd never support.
02 — Credential
Fake login
The attacker builds a page that looks exactly like your university's login page. You type your username and password into the invisible real login form underneath.
03 — Sequential
Sequence attack
The attacker maps out multiple buttons and makes you click in exactly that sequence. By the end of the game, you've completed an entire transaction you never intended.
Defenses

The Defenses — Three Generations

People tried to stop clickjacking in three different ways over the years.

Gen 1
Frame Buster Script (Dead)
Developers put a JavaScript snippet on their page that detected if it was trapped inside a foreign iframe, then broke out. Completely defeated: Attackers use the sandbox attribute on iframes, which disables JavaScript inside the iframe. The frame buster script never even starts.
Gen 2
X-Frame-Options (Deprecated)
An HTTP response header. X-Frame-Options: DENY means nobody can embed this page in an iframe. SAMEORIGIN means only the same domain can. Status: The Alice and Bob textbook explicitly warns it is deprecated. CSP is the modern replacement. Still works on old browsers but not for new builds.
Gen 3
CSP frame-ancestors (Current Standard)
A directive inside the Content-Security-Policy header. frame-ancestors 'none' = nobody can embed this page. frame-ancestors 'self' = only this domain can. This is the answer Ajan wants. Set at the web server level so it applies to every page automatically.
The smart implementation: Set CSP frame-ancestors on the web server config so it automatically applies to every single page. Not page by page, which would be a maintenance nightmare.
Concept

SOP Lives Inside iframes Too

Ajan ran a live experiment in the SEED lab to show one more thing.

Even inside iframes, the Same-Origin Policy is still active.

If your page tries to reach into a child iframe from a different domain and read its content — SOP blocks you. Access denied.

But if your page and the child iframe are both from the same domain — you can read each other's content freely.

This is why clickjacking attackers don't bother trying to read what's on the victim's page. SOP would block them. Instead they just position their invisible layer and let your click do all the work. They don't need to read the page — they just need to intercept the action.

Exam Tips

Exam / Scenario Tips

Ajan will describe a scenario — probably a website where a user gets tricked into performing an unintended action — and ask you to explain why the attack works and how to prevent it.

The biggest trap is saying the attacker "hacked" the browser. They didn't touch the browser. They used a completely legitimate HTML feature — overlapping iframes — and the browser behaved exactly as designed.

The second trap is naming X-Frame-Options as the defense. You'd get partial marks. Full marks require naming CSP frame-ancestors as the current standard, noting X-Frame-Options is deprecated and only kept for old browser compatibility, and explaining that frame buster scripts are completely dead because the sandbox attribute kills JavaScript inside iframes.

Quick Revision

Quick Revision

  • Clickjacking = two overlapping iframes. Bottom one is real and visible. Top one is invisible (opacity 0) and sits over a specific button on the bottom page. Your click lands on the invisible top layer.
  • The browser always delivers clicks to the topmost layer — this is the core mechanic the entire attack depends on
  • Stacking order is controlled by z-index (higher number = on top) or declaration order in HTML (last written = on top)
  • Three attack types Ajan taught: likejacking (fake social media clicks), fake login (credential capture), sequence of clicks (trigger a multi-step action)
  • Defense Generation 1 — Frame buster JavaScript: completely dead. Sandbox attribute on iframes disables the script.
  • Defense Generation 2 — X-Frame-Options (DENY / SAMEORIGIN): deprecated. Still works on old browsers. Not for new builds.
  • Defense Generation 3 — CSP frame-ancestors: the current correct answer. Set at the web server level so all pages are protected automatically.
  • SOP applies inside iframes too — cross-origin iframes cannot read each other's DOM content
